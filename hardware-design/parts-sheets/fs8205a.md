## What Is This Part?

The FS8205A is a pair of electronic switches in one package. Think of it as two light switches wired in series with the battery — one controls the charge path, one controls the discharge path. The DW01A protection IC tells each switch when to turn on or off.

When both switches are ON, current flows freely. When the DW01A detects danger (low battery, high battery, or short circuit), it turns the appropriate switch OFF, disconnecting the battery.

The switches are MOSFETs — a type of transistor that can handle high current with very low resistance when turned on, and completely block current when turned off.

## How MOSFETs Work

### The Basic MOSFET

MOSFET stands for **Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor Field-Effect Transistor**. Despite the intimidating name, the concept is simple:

A MOSFET has three terminals:
- **Gate (G)** — the control input (like the handle on a water faucet)
- **Source (S)** — where current enters (for N-channel)
- **Drain (D)** — where current exits

When no voltage is applied to the gate, the MOSFET is OFF — no current flows between source and drain. It's like a closed faucet. When you apply voltage to the gate (above the threshold voltage), a conducting channel forms between source and drain, and current flows freely. It's an electrically controlled switch.

The "field-effect" part means the gate controls the switch using an electric field, not by passing current. The gate is insulated from the channel by a thin layer of silicon dioxide (SiO2) — the "oxide" in MOSFET. This means the gate draws essentially zero current to maintain its state. The DW01A can hold the MOSFETs on or off with virtually no power consumption.

### N-Channel Enhancement Mode

The FS8205A uses **N-channel enhancement mode** MOSFETs:

- **N-channel** — the conducting channel is made of N-type silicon (doped with electrons as charge carriers). Current flows from drain to source. Turned ON by positive gate-to-source voltage.
- **Enhancement mode** — normally OFF. You must apply voltage to turn them on (as opposed to "depletion mode" which is normally ON).

Turn-on sequence:
1. Gate voltage rises above threshold (Vgs > Vth ≈ 0.4-1.0V)
2. Electric field from the gate attracts electrons to the surface of the silicon
3. A thin conductive layer (inversion layer) forms between source and drain
4. Current flows through this layer
5. Higher gate voltage = thicker inversion layer = lower resistance

### Rds(on) and Why It Matters

When a MOSFET is fully turned on, it's not a perfect conductor — it has a small resistance called **Rds(on)** (drain-to-source on-resistance). For the FS8205A, this is about 25 mΩ (milliohms) per MOSFET.

Why this matters: Power is dissipated as heat according to P = I²R. At 1A charge current through two MOSFETs in series:

**P = (1A)² × (25mΩ + 25mΩ) = 0.05W**

That's 50 milliwatts — negligible heat. But at higher currents (3A short-circuit current):

**P = (3A)² × 50mΩ = 0.45W**

Still manageable for a SOT-23-6 package, and the DW01A cuts the current before it gets worse.

Low Rds(on) also means minimal voltage drop. At 100mA normal operating current: V = IR = 0.1A × 0.05Ω = 5mV. The battery loses only 5mV through the protection circuit — practically invisible.

## The Back-to-Back Configuration

### Why Two MOSFETs?

A single MOSFET could disconnect the battery, but it could only block current in one direction. Lithium battery protection needs to block current in **both** directions:

- **Discharge direction** — when the battery is too low, block current flowing OUT to the load
- **Charge direction** — when the battery is too high, block current flowing IN from the charger

Each MOSFET handles one direction. MOSFET 1 (controlled by OD) blocks discharge. MOSFET 2 (controlled by OC) blocks charging. They're connected drain-to-drain (common drain) inside the package.

### The Body Diode Problem

Every MOSFET has an intrinsic **body diode** — a parasitic PN junction between the body (substrate) and drain of the transistor. This diode allows current to flow in one direction even when the MOSFET is turned off.

```
Single MOSFET:
  Source ──┤├──|◄── Drain
           MOSFET   Body diode

Even when MOSFET is OFF, current flows ◄ through body diode!
```

The back-to-back configuration solves this. With two MOSFETs in series (common drain), the body diodes point in opposite directions. When either MOSFET is OFF, its body diode blocks the other direction:

```
Back-to-back (common drain):
  S1 ──┤├──|◄──||──►|──├┤── S2
       M1   BD1  DD  BD2  M2

When M1 is OFF: BD1 blocks current from flowing S1→D
When M2 is OFF: BD2 blocks current from flowing S2→D
```

This ensures the protection circuit can truly disconnect the battery in both directions.

## How It Works with the DW01A

Normal operation (both MOSFETs ON):
```
Battery+ ── DW01A VCC ──────────────────────── Load+
Battery- ── FS8205A S2 ──[M2]──[M1]── S1 ── Load- (GND)
              Gate2=HIGH     Gate1=HIGH
```

Over-discharge (M1 OFF, M2 ON):
```
Battery+ ──────────────────────────────────── Load+
Battery- ── FS8205A S2 ──[M2]──[M1 OFF]── S1 ── Load-
              Gate2=HIGH    Gate1=LOW        DISCONNECTED
```

Over-charge (M1 ON, M2 OFF):
```
Charger+ ──────────────────────────────────── Battery+
Charger- ── S1 ──[M1]──[M2 OFF]── S2 ── Battery-
             Gate1=HIGH   Gate2=LOW    DISCONNECTED
```

## History and Background

Power MOSFETs were invented in the late 1970s (the first commercial device was the Siliconix VN10LP in 1978). The dual-MOSFET battery protection configuration became standard in the 1990s as lithium batteries entered consumer electronics. Seiko Instruments, Mitsumi, and Ricoh were early pioneers of the protection IC + dual MOSFET combination.

The FS8205A is manufactured by **Shenzhen Fuxin Semiconductor**, a Chinese company that produces MOSFET clones of popular parts from companies like Alpha & Omega Semiconductor and Diodes Incorporated. The original part it's based on is the **AO8810** (Alpha & Omega) or the **Si6968** (Vishay).

The SOT-23-6 package is remarkable — two 6A MOSFETs in a package 2.9mm long. This miniaturization is possible because the MOSFETs only carry the battery's current (typically under 1A in the Dilder), well below their 6A rating.
