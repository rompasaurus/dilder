## What Is This Part?

The TP4056 is a **dedicated lithium battery charger chip**. You feed it 5V from USB, connect a lithium battery to its output, and it handles the entire charging process automatically — constant current, constant voltage, trickle charge for deeply discharged cells, termination when full, and thermal protection if it gets too hot.

It costs 7 cents. Before chips like this existed, building a safe lithium charger required multiple ICs, an op-amp for voltage regulation, current sense resistors, and careful analog design. The TP4056 puts all of that into an 8-pin package smaller than a grain of rice.

In the Dilder, the TP4056 charges the LiPo battery from USB-C power. The battery then feeds the AMS1117-3.3 voltage regulator to power the ESP32-S3 and everything else.

## How Lithium Battery Charging Works

### The Three Charging Phases

Lithium batteries have strict rules about how they can be charged. Violate them and the battery can swell, catch fire, or explode. Every lithium charger follows the same three-phase algorithm:

**Phase 1 — Trickle Charge (Pre-conditioning)**
If the battery is deeply discharged (below ~2.9V), the charger applies a small current (typically 1/10 of the full charge current). Deeply discharged lithium cells have degraded internal chemistry. Blasting them with full current could cause lithium plating on the anode, creating internal short circuits. The trickle charge gently restores the cell to a safe voltage.

**Phase 2 — Constant Current (CC)**
Once the battery reaches ~2.9V, the charger switches to full charge current (1A in the Dilder). The charger acts as a current source — it adjusts its output voltage as needed to maintain exactly 1A flowing into the battery. During this phase, the battery voltage steadily rises from ~3.0V toward 4.2V. This phase does most of the charging (~70-80% of capacity).

**Phase 3 — Constant Voltage (CV)**
When the battery reaches 4.2V (the maximum safe voltage for lithium-ion chemistry), the charger switches from current regulation to voltage regulation. It holds the output at exactly 4.2V while the charging current naturally decreases as the battery approaches full charge. Think of it like filling a water balloon — as it gets full, the flow rate decreases even though the pressure stays constant.

The charger terminates when the current drops to about 1/10 of the programmed charge current (100mA for a 1A setting). At this point, the battery is ~95-98% full. Pushing further would stress the cell with diminishing returns.

### Why You Can't Just Apply Voltage

If you connected a 4.2V supply directly to a discharged battery:
1. The initial current would be enormous (limited only by the battery's internal resistance, typically 50-100 mOhm — that's 20-40A!)
2. This would instantly damage the battery, possibly causing thermal runaway
3. Even if the battery survived, uncontrolled charging causes lithium plating, reducing capacity and creating dendrites that can puncture the separator and short-circuit the cell

The charger's job is to **precisely control the current and voltage** at every moment.

### CC/CV Explained

"CC/CV" stands for Constant Current / Constant Voltage — the two-phase charging profile that all lithium chargers use:

```
Current (A)  |  CC Phase     |    CV Phase
    1.0 ─────|───────────────|─\
             |               |   \
             |               |    \
             |               |     \
    0.1 ─────|               |──────\──── Termination
             |               |
Voltage (V)  |               |
    4.2 ─────|           /───|────────────
             |          /    |
             |         /     |
    3.0 ─────|────────/      |
             └───────────────┴────────────  Time
```

During CC, current is held constant and voltage rises. During CV, voltage is held constant and current falls. This is the safest and most efficient way to charge lithium cells.

## How the TP4056 Implements This

### Internal Block Diagram

Inside the TP4056 is:

1. **Voltage reference** — A precision 4.2V reference (accurate to +/-1%). This sets the maximum battery voltage. The 1% tolerance matters — at 4.3V, lithium cells start to degrade rapidly; at 4.5V, they're dangerous.

2. **Error amplifier** — Compares the battery voltage to the 4.2V reference. During CC phase, the battery is below 4.2V so the error amplifier output is saturated, and the current control loop takes over. Once the battery hits 4.2V, the error amplifier takes control and regulates voltage.

3. **Current sense** — A precision resistor and mirror circuit measures charge current. The external RPROG resistor sets the reference current. The internal current mirror scales this to set the actual charge current.

4. **Pass transistor** — An internal P-channel MOSFET controls the current flow from VCC to BAT. The control loops adjust its gate voltage to maintain the target current or voltage.

5. **Thermal regulation** — A temperature sensor on-die. If junction temperature exceeds ~120°C, the charger automatically reduces charge current to prevent overheating. This means you can push the TP4056 hard and it will thermally protect itself.

6. **Timer** — An internal safety timer limits total charge time. If the battery hasn't reached termination after ~6 hours, charging stops (prevents indefinitely charging a damaged cell).

### Thermal Regulation

The TP4056 is a **linear charger**, which means it regulates by dissipating excess voltage as heat. With a 5V USB input and a 3.0V battery (worst case — deepest discharge during CC phase):

**Power dissipation = (Vin - Vbat) × Icharge = (5.0 - 3.0) × 1.0 = 2.0W**

2W in an 8-pin package is significant. This is why the ESOP-8 package (with exposed thermal pad) is used — the pad connects to the PCB ground plane, which acts as a heatsink. The thermal regulation feature will reduce charge current if the die gets too hot, so the battery charges slower but safely.

At 4.0V battery (typical midpoint), dissipation drops to (5.0 - 4.0) × 1.0 = 1.0W, which is much more manageable.

### Charge Termination

The TP4056 monitors the current during CV phase. When it drops to 1/10 of the programmed current (100mA for a 1A setting), the STDBY pin goes LOW (indicating charge complete) and the charger enters standby mode. It continues to monitor — if the battery voltage drops (due to self-discharge or load), charging resumes automatically.

## Charge Current Setting (RPROG)

A single external resistor from the PROG pin to GND sets the maximum charge current. The formula is:

**Icharge = 1200 / RPROG** (where RPROG is in Ohms and Icharge is in mA)

| RPROG Value | Charge Current | Charge Time (1000mAh) |
|-------------|----------------|-----------------------|
| **1.2k** | **1000 mA** | **~1 hour** |
| 2.0k | 580 mA | ~1.7 hours |
| 3.0k | 400 mA | ~2.5 hours |
| 5.0k | 250 mA | ~4 hours |
| 10.0k | 130 mA | ~7.7 hours |
| 20.0k | 60 mA | ~16.7 hours |

**Dilder uses R1 = 1.2k** for 1A charging of the 1000mAh battery. This gives approximately a 1-hour charge time (C-rate of 1C). The battery's datasheet should specify the maximum safe charge rate — most LiPo pouch cells can handle 1C.

## Status Indicators

| CHRG (pin 7) | STDBY (pin 6) | State |
|--------------|---------------|-------|
| LOW (LED on) | HIGH (LED off) | Charging |
| HIGH (LED off) | LOW (LED on) | Charge complete |
| HIGH (LED off) | HIGH (LED off) | No battery / standby |
| Blinking | — | Battery fault / not detected |

Both pins are open-drain — they can only pull LOW (sink current). The LEDs are connected between 3.3V (through a current-limiting resistor) and the pin. When the pin goes LOW, current flows through the LED, turning it on.

## Common Mistakes and Gotchas

1. **Don't connect TEMP to VCC** — This is a common error. TEMP should go to GND (to disable temperature monitoring) or to an NTC thermistor. Connecting it to VCC can cause the charger to refuse to charge.

2. **Input capacitor is important** — A 10uF ceramic capacitor close to VCC prevents voltage spikes during charge current changes. Without it, the TP4056 can oscillate.

3. **The TP4056 is not a power path manager** — It charges the battery; it doesn't intelligently switch between USB and battery power. In the Dilder, the SS34 Schottky diode handles power path selection.

4. **Linear charger = heat** — At 1A charge from 5V USB with a deeply discharged battery (3.0V), the chip dissipates 2W. Ensure the thermal pad is well-connected to a ground plane pour.

5. **Don't charge below 0°C** — Lithium batteries should not be charged below freezing. If the Dilder will be used in cold environments, the TEMP pin should be connected to an NTC thermistor to prevent cold charging.

## History and Background

The TP4056 is manufactured by **NanJing Top Power ASIC Corp (TPOWER)**, a Chinese semiconductor company specializing in power management ICs. The chip is a clone/equivalent of the **Microchip MCP73831**, which was one of the first single-chip lithium chargers and is still widely used in commercial products.

The TP4056 became enormously popular in the maker/hobby community because:
- It costs less than $0.10 in small quantities
- Pre-made breakout boards with USB-micro are available for ~$0.50 on AliExpress
- The CC/CV algorithm is reliable and well-understood
- It requires very few external components (just one resistor and two capacitors)

You'll find the TP4056 in countless open-source hardware projects, from Arduino battery shields to ESP32 boards to portable game consoles. The DW01A + FS8205A battery protection circuit is almost always paired with it.
