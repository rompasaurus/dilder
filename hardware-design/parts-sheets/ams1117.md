## What Is This Part?

The AMS1117-3.3 takes a messy, variable input voltage and outputs a clean, stable 3.3V. It's like a precision water pressure regulator — no matter how the water pressure fluctuates on the input side, the output stays at the same pressure.

In the Dilder, the battery voltage ranges from 4.2V (fully charged) to 3.0V (nearly dead). The ESP32-S3 needs a stable 3.3V. The AMS1117-3.3 sits between the battery and everything else, maintaining that 3.3V as the battery drains.

## How Voltage Regulators Work

### The Problem — Variable Battery Voltage

A lithium battery's voltage changes as it discharges:
- **4.2V** — fully charged
- **3.7V** — nominal (about 50% capacity remaining)
- **3.3V** — getting low
- **3.0V** — nearly empty (DW01A protection cutoff at 2.4V)

The ESP32-S3 requires 3.0V to 3.6V, with 3.3V optimal. Without regulation, a fully charged battery would feed 4.2V to the ESP32 — too high. A regulator keeps the output at exactly 3.3V regardless of input.

### Linear vs Switching Regulators

There are two fundamental approaches to voltage regulation:

**Linear regulator (like the AMS1117):** Acts like a variable resistor. It dissipates the extra voltage as heat. Simple, quiet, cheap, but wastes energy.

**Switching regulator (buck converter):** Rapidly switches the input on and off (typically at 500 kHz to 2 MHz) and uses an inductor and capacitor to smooth the output. Much more efficient (typically 85-95%) but more complex, more expensive, and generates electrical noise that can interfere with radio signals.

For the Dilder, the linear regulator was chosen because:
1. The voltage difference is small (typically 0.4V at 3.7V nominal battery)
2. The current draw is moderate (~100mA average)
3. Power wasted: (3.7 - 3.3) × 0.1 = 0.04W — negligible
4. No switching noise to interfere with WiFi/BLE radio
5. Costs $0.05 vs $0.30+ for a buck converter

### Inside a Linear Regulator

The AMS1117 contains:

1. **Bandgap voltage reference** — A precision circuit that produces a temperature-stable 1.25V reference. This uses the physics of semiconductor junctions — two transistors biased at different current densities produce voltages that change with temperature in opposite directions. Combined, they cancel out, giving a stable reference. The bandgap voltage of silicon (1.12eV) is the fundamental constant this exploits.

2. **Error amplifier** — A high-gain op-amp that compares the output voltage (divided down by an internal resistor divider to 1.25V) against the bandgap reference. Any deviation from 3.3V produces an error signal.

3. **Pass transistor** — A PNP transistor (in the AMS1117's case) that controls current flow from input to output. The error amplifier adjusts the base current to maintain exact output voltage.

4. **Thermal shutdown** — If the die temperature reaches 165°C, the regulator turns off to prevent damage.

The feedback loop works in microseconds:
- Load increases → output dips slightly → error amplifier detects the dip → drives pass transistor harder → more current flows → output returns to 3.3V

### Dropout Voltage Explained

"LDO" stands for Low Dropout. The **dropout voltage** is the minimum difference between input and output needed for the regulator to work properly.

For the AMS1117: dropout is 1.1V at full 1A load, but only 0.3V at 100mA (typical Dilder load). This means:

- At 100mA load, the battery can drop to 3.3V + 0.3V = **3.6V** before regulation is lost
- Below 3.6V, the output starts to sag below 3.3V (the regulator is "in dropout")
- The ESP32-S3 continues working down to 3.0V, so there's still some usable range

In practice, the battery delivers 3.7V for most of its discharge curve, giving comfortable headroom.

## Capacitor Requirements

The AMS1117 **requires** output capacitance for stability:

| Capacitor | Value | Ref | Position | Purpose |
|-----------|-------|-----|----------|---------|
| Input | 10uF ceramic | C5 | Close to VIN pin | Reduces input ripple, prevents oscillation |
| Output | 10uF ceramic | C6 | Close to VOUT pin | **Required for stability** — without this, the regulator oscillates |

The original AMS1117 datasheet specifies tantalum capacitors, but modern low-ESR ceramic capacitors (MLCC) work fine. The ESR (Equivalent Series Resistance) of the output capacitor affects the regulator's stability — ceramic caps have very low ESR (< 10mΩ) which the AMS1117 handles well.

## Thermal Considerations

Power dissipation: **P = (Vin - Vout) × Iload**

| Battery Voltage | Load Current | Power Dissipation |
|----------------|--------------|-------------------|
| 4.2V (full) | 100 mA | (4.2 - 3.3) × 0.1 = **90 mW** |
| 3.7V (nominal) | 100 mA | (3.7 - 3.3) × 0.1 = **40 mW** |
| 4.2V (full) | 250 mA (WiFi TX) | (4.2 - 3.3) × 0.25 = **225 mW** |

The SOT-223 package can dissipate about 1.5W with proper PCB thermal relief. At 225mW worst case, thermal management is not a concern. The tab pad should still be connected to a ground plane pour for best results.

## Why LDO Instead of a Buck Converter

| Factor | AMS1117-3.3 (LDO) | Buck Converter |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| Cost | $0.05 | $0.30-1.00 |
| External parts | 2 capacitors | Inductor + capacitors + diode |
| Efficiency (3.7V→3.3V) | ~89% | ~92% |
| Efficiency (4.2V→3.3V) | ~78% | ~92% |
| Electrical noise | None (linear) | Switching noise at 500kHz+ |
| RF interference | None | Potential WiFi/BLE interference |
| Board area | Minimal | Larger (inductor + more caps) |
| Design complexity | Trivial | Moderate (inductor selection, loop compensation) |

At the Dilder's typical 100mA load, the LDO wastes about 40mW (at 3.7V battery). A buck converter would save maybe 5-10mW but costs 6x more, needs more board space, and might degrade WiFi/BLE performance. Not worth it for this application.

## History and Background

The AMS1117 is a clone of the **Linear Technology LT1117** (now Analog Devices, since Linear Technology was acquired in 2017). Linear Technology introduced the LT1117 in the 1990s as one of the first affordable LDO regulators.

The "1117" designation has become a generic product family number — dozens of manufacturers make pin-compatible versions:
- **TI: TLV1117** (enhanced thermal performance)
- **Microchip: MCP1826** (lower dropout)
- **Diodes Inc: AP2114** (lower quiescent current)
- **AMS: AMS1117** (the one we're using, cheapest option)

The AMS1117 specifically is manufactured by **Advanced Monolithic Systems**, a semiconductor company that produces analog and mixed-signal ICs. Their version is the most commonly found in cheap development boards, breakout modules, and hobbyist projects because it costs 5 cents.

**LDO history:** Before LDO regulators, the standard was the **7805** (and its 3.3V variant 7833) — a 3-terminal regulator introduced by Fairchild Semiconductor in 1969. The 7805 has a 2V dropout, meaning it needs at least 5.3V to output a stable 3.3V. LDO regulators reduced this to under 1V, enabling battery-powered designs where every millivolt of headroom matters.
