## What Is This Part?

The SS34 is a one-way valve for electricity. Current can flow through it in one direction (anode to cathode) but is blocked in the other direction. In the Dilder, it sits between the USB-C 5V input and the TP4056 charger, serving as a power path selector:

- **USB plugged in:** The diode conducts, feeding 5V to the charger and system
- **USB unplugged:** The diode blocks, preventing battery voltage from leaking back toward the USB port

It's a "Schottky" diode, which means it has a lower voltage drop (0.55V) than a regular silicon diode (0.7V). That 0.15V difference matters when you're running from a 5V USB supply and every millivolt helps.

## How Diodes Work

### The PN Junction

A diode is made from two types of silicon placed next to each other:

- **P-type silicon** — doped with atoms like boron that have one fewer electron than silicon. This creates "holes" (missing electrons) that act as positive charge carriers.
- **N-type silicon** — doped with atoms like phosphorus that have one extra electron. These extra electrons are free to move and carry current.

Where the P and N regions meet, something interesting happens. Free electrons from the N side diffuse across the boundary and fill holes on the P side. This creates a thin zone depleted of free charge carriers — the **depletion region**. This region has a built-in electric field that opposes further diffusion.

```
  P-type    |  Depletion  |  N-type
            |   Region    |
  ○ ○ ○ ○   |  - - + +    |  ● ● ● ●
  ○ ○ ○ ○   |  - - + +    |  ● ● ● ●
  (holes)   | (no carriers)|  (electrons)
            |             |
           ◄── Electric field ──►
```

### Forward and Reverse Bias

**Forward bias** (anode positive, cathode negative): The external voltage opposes the built-in electric field, narrows the depletion region, and eventually overwhelms it (at about 0.7V for silicon). Carriers flood across the junction and current flows.

**Reverse bias** (anode negative, cathode positive): The external voltage reinforces the built-in field, widening the depletion region. No current flows (except a tiny leakage current of microamps). The diode blocks.

### Why Not Just Use a Wire?

If you connected USB VBUS directly to the battery circuit without a diode, then when USB is unplugged, the battery (3.7-4.2V) would push current backward through the USB-C connector. This could:
1. Try to power whatever is connected to the other end of the USB cable
2. Confuse the USB host controller
3. Waste battery power

The diode ensures current only flows from USB to the device, never from the device back to USB.

## What Makes a Schottky Diode Special

### Metal-Semiconductor Junction

A regular diode uses a PN junction (semiconductor-semiconductor). A Schottky diode replaces the P-type silicon with a **metal** (typically chromium, platinum, or molybdenum):

```
Regular diode:    P-silicon │ N-silicon    (PN junction)
Schottky diode:   Metal     │ N-silicon    (Metal-semiconductor junction)
```

This metal-semiconductor junction is called a **Schottky barrier** (named after German physicist Walter Schottky, who described the physics in 1938).

### Lower Forward Voltage Drop

The Schottky junction has a lower barrier height than a PN junction, meaning less voltage is needed to start conducting:

| Diode Type | Forward Voltage Drop |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Silicon PN (1N4007) | 0.65 - 0.7V |
| Schottky (SS34) | 0.45 - 0.55V |
| Germanium PN | 0.25 - 0.3V |

That 0.15V advantage means less wasted power. At 1A charge current: P = 0.55V × 1A = 0.55W for the Schottky vs 0.7V × 1A = 0.7W for a standard diode. Not huge, but it also means more voltage reaches the TP4056.

### Faster Switching

In a regular PN diode, when you switch from forward to reverse bias, the stored charge carriers (minority carriers) in the depletion region take time to recombine — this creates a brief reverse current pulse called **reverse recovery**. This takes 50-200 nanoseconds for fast PN diodes.

Schottky diodes have essentially **no reverse recovery** because there are no minority carriers — the metal contact doesn't inject them. They switch from conducting to blocking in under 1 nanosecond. This matters in high-frequency circuits (switching power supplies, RF) more than in the Dilder's DC application, but it's free.

## How It's Used in the Dilder

```
USB-C VBUS (5V) ──►|── SS34 ──── VBUS_CHG ──── TP4056 VCC
                   D1  (anode)   (cathode)
                   
When USB is connected:  5V - 0.5V = 4.5V reaches TP4056
When USB is disconnected: D1 blocks battery from feeding back to USB
```

This is sometimes called a "power OR" configuration — the system is powered by whichever source provides higher voltage. When USB is connected (5V > battery 3.7V), USB power flows through the diode. When USB is removed, the battery feeds the LDO directly (bypassing the TP4056 output).

| Pin | Function | Connect To | Net |
|-----|----------|------------|-----|

**Polarity matters!** The band marking on the SMA package indicates the cathode (output side). Install it backwards and USB power won't reach the charger.

## History and Background

The Schottky diode is named after **Walter Hans Schottky** (1886-1976), a German physicist who worked at Siemens. He described the metal-semiconductor junction physics in 1938, though the effect had been observed since the early days of radio (the "cat's whisker" detector in crystal radios was essentially a crude Schottky diode, using a metal wire touching a galena crystal).

The "SS34" part number follows a common naming convention:
- **SS** = Schottky Surface-mount
- **3** = 3A current rating
- **4** = 40V reverse voltage

Variants: SS32 (20V), SS34 (40V), SS36 (60V), SS54 (5A/40V), etc.

The SMA package (also called DO-214AC) is one of the most common surface-mount diode packages. It handles up to 3A with adequate thermal dissipation to a PCB pad. For the Dilder's typical 1A current, the package is generously rated.
